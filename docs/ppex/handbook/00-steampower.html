Pipes and Power Expanded adds pipe networks and modular steam machinery. This way you can produce mechanical power just from boiling water, but it is expensive in terms of materials and may lead to catastrophic events! So be sure you are well established and has achieved iron age before you attempt to build your first boiler.
The general ides is for you to route everything as: water supply == boiler == engine == sub-machine. So you need to build a working loop, that will feed itself, while burning coal and producing the power you want.
This article covers only the basic pipes that connect everything, see Steam Power: Boilers, Steam Power: Engines and Steam Power: Fittings for the information about the machines.

How pipe networks work
Connected pipes form one network with a shared pool. A network carries one medium at a time — a gas (air, blast, exhaust, steam) or water, never both. Each pipe adds 30 L of capacity, which get's added together for total network capacity. Pressure is simply volume over capacity, shown in atm (atmosphere). The whole network also shares one temperature.

Materials matter. An iron pipe bursts above 5 atm, a steel pipe is stronger and can take pressure up to 10 atm, and the weakest pipe limits the whole run. An over-pressured run holds for some time, then pipes can start to break.

Open ends leak. Any unconnected pipe connector bleeds the network (≈8 L/s of gas, 10 L/s of water), so a leaking run can never build pressure (leaking pipe cannot accumulate pressure past 1atm). Cap runs against machine ports, valves or outlets. Standing water in pipes and boilers also slowly evaporates (≈50 L per day).

Building runs. Straights, bends, T- and X-junctions snap their orientation to neighbouring pipes when placed; rotate a piece with a wrench if it guessed wrong. Pipes connect to machines through their port faces (boiler outlets, engine inlets, pump and intake flanges) — butt the pipe straight against the port.
